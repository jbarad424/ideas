The Problem

What Small Ecommerce Businesses Actually Deal With

If you sell on Amazon FBA or Shopify and make between $200K and $2M per year, your bookkeeping situation probably looks like this: you pay someone $300 to $800 per month to do work that is 80% data entry. They download your Amazon settlement PDF, manually break out fees, type numbers into QuickBooks, categorize your bank transactions, and send you a P&L weeks after the month closes. If you have a question about your numbers, you email them and wait.

The problem isn't that bookkeepers are bad at their jobs. The problem is that most of what they do can be automated with tools that already exist. Amazon has an API that gives you every fee line item. QuickBooks has bank rules that auto-categorize transactions. Make.com can watch your email and create invoices automatically. The tools are there. Nobody wires them together.

"I don't know my actual margins until three weeks after the month ends."
Automated settlement processing means your Amazon fees, ad costs, and net revenue post to QuickBooks within hours of a settlement, not weeks.
"My bookkeeper keeps asking me what a transaction is."
Bank rules + an AI exception queue handle this. Known transactions get auto-categorized. Unknowns get flagged with an AI best guess for you to confirm, not research from scratch.
"I'm paying $600/month and most of it is just data entry."
That's the core issue. A one-time automation setup replaces the data entry. You keep your CPA for taxes and strategy. You stop paying someone monthly to type numbers.
"I've looked at Finaloop, A2X, Webgility, but I still need a human."
SaaS tools sync data, but they don't configure your specific chart of accounts, set up rules for your specific vendors, or handle the edge cases. That's what this service does.
Proof of Concept

What I Actually Built for My Own Business

Chubby Buttons LLC is an ecommerce brand selling on Amazon FBA and Shopify. We have three vendors (manufacturing, ads, fulfillment), two bank accounts, and previously paid HCA Consulting about $500/month for bookkeeping. Here's what replaced them:

Automation 1

Amazon Settlement Processing

Amazon's SP-API feeds settlement data into a webhook that breaks out 16 fee categories. Make.com creates an 11-line journal entry in QuickBooks every two weeks. No human involved.

Automation 2

Vendor Invoice Capture

Gmail watchers monitor three inboxes for vendor emails. AI parses the invoice, Make.com creates a draft Bill in QuickBooks with the correct vendor and expense account pre-filled.

Automation 3

Transaction Categorization

16 QuickBooks bank rules auto-categorize recurring transactions: subscriptions, marketplace deposits, payroll, utilities. Covers 90%+ of monthly volume without any human review.

Automation 4

Exception Handling

Transactions the AI can't confidently categorize go to a Notion-based exception queue. Owner gets an email with the AI's best guess. They confirm or correct. Answer flows back to QuickBooks.

The system runs on Make.com ($20/mo), a Render webhook ($7/mo), and QuickBooks Online (already paid for). Total new cost: roughly $500/year. The CPA (Southard Accountancy) stays for taxes. Everything else is automated.

The Engagement

How the Service Would Actually Work

This is implementation consulting, not software. I come in, understand your specific business, wire up the automations, prove they work against your real data, and hand you documentation. Then I either leave or stay on a light retainer.

Discovery call (free, 30 min)

Understand the business: what do you sell, where do you sell it, who are your vendors, what does your bookkeeper do today, what does your QuickBooks look like. Determine if automation is a fit.

Audit and plan (Week 1)

Review QuickBooks chart of accounts, bank feeds, current categorization. Map every recurring transaction type. Identify what can be automated vs. what needs human judgment. Deliver a written plan.

Build (Weeks 2-3)

Configure bank rules, build Make.com automation scenarios, connect APIs (Amazon SP-API, Shopify connector, Gmail watchers), set up exception queue. All built and tested against real transaction data.

Parallel testing (Weeks 4-6)

Run the automation alongside the existing bookkeeper for one full month. Compare results. Fix any discrepancies. This is the proof that the system works before anyone gets cut.

Handoff and documentation (Week 7)

Written SOP for the monthly routine. Loom walkthrough videos. The owner (or their VA) inherits a 30-minute monthly checklist. Existing bookkeeper transitions off or scales down.

Optional: monthly retainer

Monitor automation health, handle exceptions, adjust for new vendors or accounts, provide a monthly AI-generated financial summary. Light touch, not a full bookkeeping engagement.

Important Distinctions

What This Service Is and Isn't

This IS

Implementation consulting: wiring up existing tools (QuickBooks, Make.com, Amazon API, Gmail) for a specific business. Building the plumbing so the data flows automatically. Handing off a working system with documentation.

This is NOT

A software product competing with Finaloop or A2X. Not a bookkeeping firm. Not a CPA or tax service. Not a replacement for strategic financial advice. The client's existing CPA stays for taxes.

On licensing: No CPA or special license is required. Bookkeeping services in the US don't require government certification. This is tech consulting that touches financial systems. Professional liability insurance (E&O, roughly $500-$1,000/year) is recommended.

On security: Clients grant QuickBooks admin access the same way they give it to any bookkeeper. API credentials are created under the client's own Amazon or Shopify accounts. No bank passwords are ever shared.

Market Timing

Why Now

Right now, AI tools like Claude are powerful enough to parse invoices, categorize transactions, and analyze financial data. Make.com can orchestrate complex workflows across APIs. QuickBooks handles the system of record. But these tools are not yet easy enough for a non-technical business owner to set up themselves.

That gap between "the tools exist" and "people can use them" is the window. In 12 to 24 months, it will get easier. The move now is: build real implementations, collect case studies, and be the person people trust to do this work.
