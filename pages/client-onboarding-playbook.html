Client Onboarding Playbook

How to replicate the Chubby Buttons bookkeeping automation for a new client. Based on real lessons from the first build.

Before You Start: What to Ask the Client

Every engagement starts with understanding what the client's accountant or CPA currently produces. Work backwards from the deliverable.

  1. What does your accountant send you each month? Get a copy of their actual work product (financial package, reports, spreadsheets). This is your target output.
  2. What accounting software do you use? QuickBooks Online is ideal (good API). Xero also works. Desktop software is harder.
  3. Where do you sell? Amazon, Shopify, direct, wholesale? Each sales channel has different data feeds and reconciliation needs.
  4. What bank and credit cards? Need to know the accounts, last 4 digits, and whether bank feeds are connected in QBO.
  5. Who does what today? Map every manual step their accountant performs. This becomes your automation checklist.
  6. What's your chart of accounts? Pull it from QBO. Your automation will reference these exact names.
Key Lesson from Chubby Buttons

Don't try to replace the accountant on day one. Run in parallel for 2-3 months. Let the automation generate the same output, compare it against what the accountant produces, and fix discrepancies. The accountant becomes your QA team.

The Build Phases

Phase 1: QuickBooks Foundation 1-2 sessions

Set up QBO API access (OAuth2). Design bank rules for recurring transactions (subscriptions, known vendors, platform deposits). Have the client's accountant create the rules in QBO since the API doesn't support rule creation.

Phase 2: Sales Channel Pipelines 2-4 sessions per channel

Build data pipelines for each sales channel. For Amazon: SP-API settlement webhook that aggregates fees and creates journal entries. For Shopify: use the native Intuit connector. Each channel needs its own reconciliation logic and clearing account.

Phase 3: Transaction Categorization 1-2 sessions

Three layers: (1) QBO bank rules for known patterns, (2) AI review with Claude for everything else, (3) Exception Queue for items that need human judgment. The AI categorizer needs the client's chart of accounts and business context baked into its system prompt.

Phase 4: Financial Package Generator 3-5 sessions

Build the output workbook. Study the client's existing financial package tab by tab. Replicate each tab using QBO API data (Trial Balance, P&L, Balance Sheet, General Ledger, reconciliation tabs). This is the most time-intensive phase.

Phase 5: Monthly Close Workflow 1-2 sessions

Wire up the approval-gated flow: checklist in Notion, statement upload detection, human approval gate, automated FP generation. This is mostly Make.com scenario building and Notion database design.

Phase 6: Parallel Run 2-3 months

Run the automation alongside the existing accountant. Compare outputs monthly. Fix discrepancies. The client's accountant handles QBO verification, month-end adjustments, and flags anything that looks off. Gradually reduce their involvement as confidence builds.

Lessons Learned

Don't touch the Chart of Accounts

Your automation should adapt to the client's existing account structure, not the other way around. If you rename accounts, you'll break the accountant's workflow and any existing reports.

Sales Tax is Always Messy

Amazon's settlement data and their tax reports show different numbers for the same period. This is because the settlement API includes refund tax adjustments and other granular items that the tax reports net out. Plan for a month-end sales tax reconciliation step. Don't try to make your automation perfectly match the tax report.

The Accountant is Your Ally, Not Your Competitor

Position the automation as handling data entry so the accountant can focus on review and judgment. They'll be skeptical at first ("we cannot rely on automation") but will come around once they see the numbers match for 2-3 months straight.

Ground Rules for QBO Are Critical

Send a clear email to everyone with QBO access: don't rename accounts, don't edit automated journal entries, don't modify bank rules without checking with you first. Everything else is fair game. This prevents conflicts between manual and automated work.

Build the Exception Queue Early

You'll never categorize 100% of transactions automatically. Having a clean escalation path (AI flags it, human answers a plain English question, system applies the answer) is more important than trying to make the AI perfect. Over time, the exception rate drops as you add bank rules for recurring patterns.

The Approval Gate Prevents Disasters

Never auto-generate the financial package without human sign-off. A simple checklist with an approval step means someone has verified that all the pieces are in place before the output is created. This is what makes the client comfortable.

Prior Period Timing Differences Are Not Worth Fixing

Amazon settlements cover date ranges that don't align with calendar months. A March 13 settlement might include 2 days of February sales. Don't create adjusting entries for this. Just be consistent (book when the settlement arrives) and the month-to-month comparisons will be valid.

Infrastructure per Client

Based on the Chubby Buttons build:

  • Render.com: ~$14/month for 2 services (settlement webhook + FP generator)
  • Make.com: Shared across clients (10-12 scenarios per client, ~1000-3000 ops/month)
  • Notion: Free tier works for databases (Exception Queue, Checklist, Inventory)
  • Claude API: Minimal cost for transaction categorization (~$1-5/month per client depending on volume)
  • Total incremental cost per client: ~$15-20/month

What You Need from the Client

  • QBO admin access (for OAuth2 app setup)
  • A copy of their current financial package (the target output)
  • Their chart of accounts
  • Bank/CC statement PDFs (for reconciliation tab design)
  • Access to sales channel APIs (Amazon SP-API, Shopify, etc.)
  • An accountant or bookkeeper willing to run in parallel for 2-3 months
  • A Google Drive folder for statement uploads (or similar)
  • A Notion workspace (or willingness to use one for the Exception Queue and checklists)
