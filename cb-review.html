How We Make the Photos
The Approach That WorksIN PRODUCTION
We show the AI a real photo of the CB2 and say "put this product on a person doing [activity]." The AI generates a new lifestyle scene with the CB2 on the person's arm. We generate 5-10 versions of each scene and pick the best ones.
178 images reviewed so far. Best scores: 98, 97, 94, 93 out of 100. Works great for hiking, skiing, and motorcycle. Still struggling with MTB and snowmobile — the AI gets confused by bike handlebars and bulky snowmobile gear.
The #1 Problem: Buttons Face the Wrong Way
Fixed: Use a Different Reference PhotoPROVEN
The old reference photo was taken looking down at your own arm (wearer's view). The AI copied that angle, so the buttons appeared flipped to anyone looking at the photo. Fix: We switched to a photo of J&Mike standing together — taken by someone ELSE looking at their arms from the outside.
A/B test results: Old photo = 0% correct button orientation. New J&Mike photo = 40-60% correct, with scores up to a perfect 100. Justin also figured out that which ARM matters — left arm and right arm need opposite button layouts because you read them left-to-right from the wearer's perspective. 20 new images using this fix are in the TO REVIEW tab now.
Built: Auto-Fix for Remaining Flipped ImagesREADY TO USE
For the 40-60% of images where the buttons still come out wrong, we built an automatic fixer. An AI finds just the CB2 in the photo, cuts it out, rotates it 180 degrees, and pastes it back with smooth edges. Costs half a cent per fix.
Tested and working. The detection AI cleanly finds just the device (not the strap or the arm), and the rotation looks natural because the CB2 is small enough that lighting differences are invisible. Script is ready to run on any image.
What We Tried and Why We Stopped
Edge Detection + Product Matching (Pipeline G)DIDN'T WORK
The idea: Use an outline drawing of the CB2 to force the AI to draw it the right way, while also showing it a photo so it knows what it looks like. Why it failed: The two systems fight each other — when both are active, the image turns into a garbled mess. Also, this only works on an older, lower-quality AI model that costs 2.5x more.
Tested with 4 real API calls. Every combination produced worse results than what we already have. Abandoned.
Painting the CB2 Into Existing Scenes (Pipelines B, C, E)0-5 / 100
The idea: Generate a nice outdoor scene first, then "paint" the CB2 into a specific spot on the person's arm. Tried 3 versions: rough mask, precise mask with text description, and precise mask with a reference photo. Why it failed: The AI can't paint a recognizable CB2 from a text description alone, and when you give it a reference photo, it warps the entire image instead of just filling the masked area.
18 tests across all 3 approaches. Highest score: 5 out of 100. The "painting in" approach is fundamentally limited by how inpainting works in current AI models.
Scene-Swapping Real Photos (Pipeline F)NICE BUT LIMITED
The idea: Start with one of our real product photos and tell the AI "keep the CB2 exactly as-is but change the background to a mountain trail." Why we stopped: It works for single images but every result looks like the original modeling photo with a new backdrop — not enough variety for a full marketing campaign.
Best score: 80/100. Good for quick one-offs, but can't rotate the buttons or create truly new compositions. Not scalable.
Teaching the AI What CB2 Looks Like (LoRA Training)WORTH RETRYING
The idea: Fine-tune the AI on photos of the actual CB2 so it "learns" the product and can draw it from memory. What went wrong: We tried 8 times, but every training run had bad data — one version literally trained on a broken download (an error page, not photos), another used only tabletop product shots so the AI learned "CB2 on a table" instead of "CB2 on a person doing sports."
The concept isn't dead — we just never gave it good data. We now have 87 great third-person lifestyle photos from the Moto and Tahoe shoots. A new training run with these photos could teach the AI to draw the CB2 accurately in action scenes. Plan is ready, waiting for the right moment to pull the trigger.
AI Scoring (Replacing Human Reviews)ABANDONED
The idea: Have another AI rate the images so Justin doesn't have to review every one. Why it failed: The AI gave 5/5 to images where the product was literally floating in mid-air. It can't tell the difference between "looks like a photo" and "the CB2 is accurate."
Justin's eye is irreplaceable for product accuracy. That's why this review page exists.
What We Know Works
Generate Many, Pick the BestCORE STRATEGY
The exact same prompt with different random seeds produces wildly different results — from 5/100 to 98/100. The AI is unpredictable image-to-image, but consistently produces winners if you generate enough.
Strategy: generate 5-10 versions of each scene, cherry-pick the best. This is how we've gotten every 90+ image.
Waist-Up Framing + Simple PromptsPROVEN
Full body shots make the CB2 too small to see. Extreme close-ups lose the scene. Waist-up is the sweet spot — you see the person, the activity, AND the product. Short, direct prompts work better than long, detailed ones.
Every image scoring 90+ uses waist-up framing with a 1-2 sentence prompt.
